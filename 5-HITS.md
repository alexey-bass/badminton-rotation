# Badminton Doubles — Shots (Hits)

## Shot Classification

Every shot in badminton falls into one of two categories:

- **Offensive shots** — shuttle travels **downward** or flat. You're attacking.
- **Defensive shots** — shuttle travels **upward**. You're giving the opponent a chance to attack.

The golden rule of doubles: **keep the shuttle going down.** Every upward shot puts your pair on defense.

---

## 1. Smash

### The King of Doubles

The smash is the most powerful shot in badminton — a steep downward strike designed to end the rally or force a very weak return. It is the **primary attacking weapon** in doubles.

### Types of Smash

#### Full Smash

- Maximum power, steep angle downward.
- Hit at the highest possible contact point with full arm and wrist snap.
- Speed can exceed 300+ km/h at professional level.
- **When to use:** you're behind the shuttle, balanced, and ready. Default attacking shot.

#### Half-Smash

- About 60–70% power of a full smash.
- Slightly less steep angle.
- **When to use:** as a **variation** after several full smashes. Changes the pace and catches the defender off-guard. Also useful when you're not perfectly positioned for a full smash.
- Advantage: easier to recover from, less tiring.

#### Stick Smash (Tap Smash)

- A quick, compact smash using mainly wrist and forearm — minimal backswing.
- Less power but much **faster preparation** and more deceptive.
- **When to use:** when you need to be quick rather than powerful. Front/mid-court intercepts.

#### Jump Smash

- The player jumps and hits the shuttle at the highest possible point.
- Creates the steepest angle and most power.
- **When to use:** when the lift is high enough to warrant jumping. Very effective but tiring and risky (poor balance on landing). Use selectively.

#### Slice Smash

- The racket face contacts the shuttle at an angle, adding spin/slice.
- The shuttle curves and drops faster than expected.
- **When to use:** as deception — the shuttle path is different from a standard smash, confusing the defender's timing.

### Smash Technique

1. Turn sideways, non-racket shoulder pointing at the net.
2. Raise the non-racket arm to "aim" at the shuttle (helps timing and balance).
3. Pull the racket arm back (like throwing a javelin).
4. Rotate the body, transfer weight forward.
5. Extend the arm and snap the wrist at the **highest contact point.**
6. Follow through — the racket continues across the body.
7. **Exhale** on contact — helps generate power.

### Smash Placement in Doubles

| Target | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Straight** | Fastest path, hardest to defend. Default choice. |
| **Body** | Aim at the defender's hip/chest. Jams their movement. |
| **Middle** | Between both defenders. Creates confusion about who takes it. |
| **Cross-court** | Use **rarely** — slower trajectory, easier to read. Only when defender is clearly out of position. |

### Important Notes

- **Don't smash everything.** If you're off-balance or late to the shuttle, a drop or clear is safer.
- **Consecutive smashes are tactically sound.** Don't feel you need to alternate — 4–5 smashes in a row is perfectly fine.
- **Smashing costs energy and balance.** Make sure your partner can cover if the block comes back fast.

## 2. Drop Shot

### The Deception Shot

The drop shot sends the shuttle **gently just over the net** from the rear court. Its wind-up looks identical to a smash or clear, making it a powerful weapon of deception.

### Types of Drop

#### Fast Drop

- Steeper angle, more speed.
- Lands further from the net but arrives faster.
- Less deceptive but harder to reach in time.
- **When to use:** when speed is more important than placement.

#### Slow Drop (Soft Drop)

- Very gentle touch — the shuttle barely clears the net and drops tight.
- Lands very close to the net.
- Highly deceptive but riskier (may not clear the net).
- **When to use:** when the opponent is deep in their court, expecting a smash.

#### Reverse Slice Drop

- Uses a slicing motion that makes the shuttle spin and drop steeply.
- The arm action looks like a smash but the shuttle drops near the net.
- Very deceptive — advanced shot.
- **When to use:** when you want maximum deception from a smash-like setup.

### Drop Shot Technique

1. **Setup must look identical to a smash.** Same body turn, same arm position, same preparation.
2. At the last moment, **reduce power** — slow the wrist snap or angle the racket face.
3. Guide the shuttle over the net with control rather than power.
4. **Follow the drop forward** — move toward the net to cover the front.

### When to Use in Doubles

- As a **variation** during an attack sequence (smash-smash-drop catches the defender off-guard).
- When you're **not positioned well enough for a quality smash.**
- To **pull both defenders forward**, opening the rear court for a clear or smash on the next shot.
- **Don't overuse** — drops rarely win points outright in doubles. They set up the next shot.

## 3. Drive

### The Pressure Shot

The drive is a fast, flat shot that travels horizontally just above the net. It's the **most common shot during flat exchanges** and is used heavily in doubles to maintain tempo and pressure.

### Types of Drive

#### Flat Drive

- Horizontal trajectory, minimal arc.
- Travels fast and low across the net.
- **When to use:** most drive exchanges. The default.

#### Offensive Drive

- Slightly descending trajectory — the shuttle goes slightly downward.
- **When to use:** when you're in a good position and want to increase attacking pressure.

#### Defensive Drive

- Slightly ascending trajectory — the shuttle goes slightly upward.
- **When to use:** as a counter to a smash. A flat drive that passes the front player can flip the situation from defense to attack.

#### Top-Spin Drive

- A drive with a follow-through slice that adds topspin.
- The shuttle initially rises then flattens out.
- **When to use:** advanced defensive counter — the spin makes it harder for the opponent to attack downward.

### Drive Technique

1. Racket face **straight ahead**, locked wrist.
2. Short, sharp motion — use forearm rotation and wrist snap.
3. Contact the shuttle in front of your body.
4. Minimal backswing — speed comes from the wrist, not the arm.
5. Can be played forehand or backhand.

### Drive Targets in Doubles

| Target | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Straight** | Past the front player into the rear court. If it passes, rotate to attack. |
| **At the body** | Between the forehand and backhand of the opponent. Forces a weak return. |
| **To the gap** | Between both opponents. Creates hesitation. |

### Doubles Context

- Drives are the **bread and butter of mid-court exchanges.**
- A fast drive battle tests reflexes and nerve — the pair that keeps the shuttle lower wins.
- If the opponent's drive rises above net height → **intercept with a kill** at the front.

## 4. Clear (Lob/Lift)

### The Reset Shot

The clear sends the shuttle high and deep to the opponent's baseline. In doubles, it is a **defensive shot** — use it to buy time when you're under pressure, but understand that it gives the opponent the attack.

### Types of Clear

#### Defensive Clear (High Clear)

- Very high arc, deep to the baseline.
- Gives you maximum time to recover and get into position.
- **When to use:** when you're completely out of position and need time to reset.

#### Attacking Clear (Punch Clear)

- Lower, flatter trajectory — still clears the opponent but arrives faster.
- Gives the opponent less time to prepare.
- **When to use:** when the opponent has failed to cover a rear corner. Rare in doubles.

### Clear Technique

1. Get behind/under the shuttle.
2. Full arm extension upward.
3. Contact at the highest point.
4. Follow through in the direction of the shot.
5. For defensive clears: aim for maximum height and depth.

### When to Use in Doubles

- **As little as possible.** Every clear gives your opponent a smash opportunity.
- When you absolutely cannot keep the shuttle going down or flat.
- After lifting, **immediately** switch to side-by-side defensive formation.

## 5. Net Shot

### The Setup Shot

A soft, controlled shot played close to the net. In doubles, the net shot is one of the most important tools — a tight net shot **forces the opponent to lift**, which gives your partner the attack.

### Types of Net Shot

#### Straight Net Shot (Tumbling/Spinning)

- The shuttle tumbles/spins as it barely clears the net.
- Lands very close to the net on the opponent's side.
- The spin makes it extremely difficult to control the return.
- **When to use:** default net shot. Aim for the shuttle to just barely cross the net.

#### Cross-Court Net Shot

- Travels diagonally across the net to the opposite side.
- Creates a wider angle, forcing the opponent to move further.
- Riskier — longer travel distance means more chance of error or interception.
- **When to use:** when the straight net is heavily covered.

#### Net Lift (Defensive Net Clear)

- From the net, the shuttle is pushed high and deep.
- This is a defensive net shot — it gives the opponent the attack.
- **When to use:** when you can't play a tight net shot (shuttle too low) and have no other option.

### Net Shot Technique

1. Get to the shuttle as **early as possible** — before it drops too low.
2. Gentle push with the fingers and wrist — almost no arm movement.
3. The closer the shuttle drops to the net, the better — but the riskier.
4. **Don't flick the wrist** — smooth, controlled motion.
5. Slice the shuttle slightly for spin (tumbling net shot).

### Doubles Context

- A good net shot **forces a lift** → your partner at the back can smash.
- After playing a net shot, **stay at the net** — threaten the net kill if the opponent plays a weak net reply.
- The **front player in attack formation** lives and dies by net shots and net kills.

## 6. Net Kill

### The Finishing Shot

The net kill is a sharp, downward strike at the net used to end the rally immediately. It punishes any shuttle that rises above net height near the front of the court.

### Technique

1. Racket held **high** — ready position is above net height.
2. As the shuttle rises above the net, **push it down steeply.**
3. Minimal backswing — it's a **tap**, not a swing.
4. Contact point: **as close to the top of the net as possible** without touching it.
5. **Careful not to touch the net** — that's a fault.

### When to Use

- Whenever the opponent plays a net shot that is **too high** (above net tape).
- After a serve that's too loose.
- On a weak block that floats above the net.
- **Always take a net kill when it's available.** There is no reason not to.

### Net Kill Targets

- **Straight down** — steepest angle, hardest to reach.
- **At the body** of the nearest opponent.
- **Into the gap** between opponents.
- Avoid hitting too wide — the shuttle might go out.

## 7. Push

### The In-Between Shot

A push is a flat-to-slightly-downward shot played from the front or mid-court, aimed at the gap between or behind the opponents. It's faster than a net shot but not as powerful as a smash.

### Technique

- Quick wrist flick from the front court.
- Aim for mid-court areas — between the opponents or behind the front player.
- Can be straight or cross-court.

### When to Use in Doubles

- When the shuttle is near net height (not high enough for a kill, not low enough for a net shot).
- To **break up the opponent's formation** — a push to mid-court forces someone to lift.
- When the opponent is rushing forward to claim the net — push behind them.
- As a return of serve: push to the body of the server's partner.

---

## 8. Block

### The Defensive Counter

A block is a short, controlled return of a smash. The shuttle drops just over the net — essentially a defensive net shot played against a powerful attacking shot.

### Technique

1. Racket in front of the body, grip relaxed.
2. **Absorb** the power of the smash — don't swing, just present the racket face.
3. Angle the racket to direct the shuttle just over the net.
4. Keep the shuttle **tight to the net** — a high block will be killed.
5. The quality of the block depends on how well you read the smash.

### Types of Block

| Type | Description | Result |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Straight block** | Returns the shuttle straight back, tight to the net | Forces the smasher's front player to lift or play a net shot |
| **Cross-court block** | Redirects the smash diagonally across the net | Catches the front player out of position but riskier |
| **Deep block** | A slightly longer block that lands mid-court | Less effective — gives opponent another attacking chance |

### Doubles Context

- A tight block is the **#1 way to transition from defense to attack.**
- After a successful block, the blocker should **move forward to the net** → rotation from side-by-side to front-back.
- Practice blocks relentlessly — they are the most important defensive skill in doubles.

## 9. Fake Shots (Deception)

### Fake Smash → Drop

- Wind up as if smashing, but at the last moment play a soft drop.
- Very effective after several real smashes — the defender prepares for power and gets finesse.

### Fake Drop → Smash

- Start the motion slowly as if playing a drop, then accelerate into a smash.
- Requires advanced racket control.

### Fake Net Shot → Push

- Approach the shuttle as if playing a tight net shot, then push it flat behind the front player.
- Catches the opponent moving forward when you send it back.

### Key to Deception

- Your **preparation must look identical** for different shots. Same stance, same arm position, same backswing.
- Deception only works if you have consistent basic shots first. If your smash looks different from your drop in the setup, the deception fails.
- **Don't overuse deception** — if you fake too often, your opponents stop reacting to your setup and just watch the shuttle.

## 10. Shot Selection Summary for Doubles

### From the Rear Court (Back Player in Attack)

| Priority | Shot | When |
|----------|------|------|
| 1 | **Smash** | Default. Use it frequently. |
| 2 | **Fast drop** | Variation after smashes. Pull defenders forward. |
| 3 | **Half-smash** | Change of pace. Less tiring. |
| 4 | **Slow drop** | Maximum deception when opponent expects power. |
| 5 | **Attacking clear** | Only when opponent leaves a rear corner open. Very rare. |

### From the Front Court (Front Player in Attack)

| Priority | Shot | When |
|----------|------|------|
| 1 | **Net kill** | Shuttle above net height. Always take it. |
| 2 | **Tight net shot** | Shuttle below net height. Forces a lift → partner smashes. |
| 3 | **Push** | Near net height. Opponent trying to claim the net. |
| 4 | **Drive** | Shuttle at/slightly above net height. Quick flat attack. |
| 5 | **Lift** | Absolute last resort. You're in deep trouble. |

### From Defense (Side-by-Side)

| Priority | Shot | When |
|----------|------|------|
| 1 | **Block** | Tight to the net. Transitions you to attack. |
| 2 | **Flat drive** | Counter-smash past the front player. High risk/reward. |
| 3 | **Push to body** | Jams the smasher's next shot. |
| 4 | **Cross-court lift** | When opponent smashes cross, lift straight. |
| 5 | **High lift** | Safe but keeps you in defense. Use to survive and reset. |
