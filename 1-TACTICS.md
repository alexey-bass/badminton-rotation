# Badminton Doubles — Tactics & Strategies

## Core Principle

The fundamental goal in doubles is to **force your opponents to hit the shuttle upward** (lift/clear), giving your side the opportunity to attack downward (smash/drop). The pair that controls whether the shuttle goes up or down controls the rally.

---

## 1. The First Three Shots

The serve, return of serve, and third shot decide the outcome of **40–60% of rallies** at amateur level. Mastering these three shots is the single biggest tactical improvement you can make.

### Serving Side Strategy

- **Default serve: low and short** to the T (center line junction). This limits the receiver's attacking angles.
- **Vary occasionally** with flick serves (wide or straight) to keep the receiver honest. If you never flick, the receiver will creep forward and dominate your low serve.
- **Communicate with your partner** before each serve — use hand signals behind the back to indicate serve type and placement. The partner must know what's coming to position for the third shot.
- **Server's job after serving:** stay at the net area, ready to intercept weak returns. You are the front player.
- **Partner's job:** stand ~2 racket lengths behind the server, in mid-court. Ready to drive, intercept, or smash depending on the return.

### Receiving Side Strategy

- **Priority: do NOT lift.** A lift hands the attack to the serving side.
- **Best returns:** net shot (tight, forces them to lift), push to mid-court gap (between the two opponents), or flat drive past the front player.
- **Net kill** on a loose serve — punish immediately.
- **Lift/clear only as last resort** — if the serve is too good to attack, lift high and deep, then get into defensive formation fast.

### Third Shot

- If the return was a net shot → server or partner goes for a net kill or tight net reply.
- If the return was a push/drive → partner at mid-court intercepts with a drive or drop.
- If the return was a lift → partner smashes.
- The third shot is where rallies are often won or lost. The serving pair should have a **pre-planned response** for each type of return.

## 2. Attacking Tactics

### When to Attack

You are attacking whenever your opponents hit the shuttle upward. Immediately shift to front-back formation.

### Shot Selection from the Rear Court

| Shot | When to Use | Risk Level |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Straight smash** | Default attacking shot. Fastest, hardest to defend. | Low |
| **Smash to the body** | Target the hip/shoulder of the defender. Hard to return cleanly. | Low |
| **Smash to the middle** | Between both defenders. Creates confusion about who takes it. | Low |
| **Cross-court smash** | Only when opponent is clearly out of position. Slower trajectory. | High |
| **Half-smash / slice** | Change of pace. Catches defenders expecting full power. | Medium |
| **Fast drop** | Variation to pull defenders forward. Sets up the next smash. | Medium |
| **Slow drop** | Greater deception but gives opponents more time. | Medium-High |
| **Attacking clear** | Only when opponents fail to cover a rear corner. Very rare in doubles. | High |

### Shot Selection from the Front Court

| Shot | When to Use |
|------|-------------|
| **Net kill** | Whenever shuttle is above net height at the front. Always take it. |
| **Tight net shot** | When shuttle is below net height. Forces a lift → partner smashes. |
| **Push to mid-court** | When opponent is trying to claim the net. Aim between/behind them. |
| **Drive** | When shuttle is near net height. Quick flat shot at the opponents. |

### Key Attacking Principles

1. **Smash more than you drop.** Four smashes in a row is tactically sound. Drops are variations, not defaults.
2. **Change the pace.** Alternate full smash → half-smash → drop → full smash. Monotonous attacks are easy to read.
3. **Change the angle.** Straight → middle → cross (rarely). Don't be predictable.
4. **The front player dictates rotation.** If the front player doesn't move, the back player can't cover gaps. The front player's movement tells the back player what to cover.
5. **Don't overcommit.** Every smash costs balance. Make sure your partner can cover if the block comes back fast.

## 3. Defensive Tactics

### When to Defend

You are defending whenever you hit the shuttle upward (lift/lob/clear). Immediately shift to side-by-side formation.

### Defensive Shot Options

| Shot | Purpose | Outcome |
|------|---------|---------|
| **High lift to baseline** | Buy time, reset the rally. | Safe but keeps you in defense. |
| **Block to net** | Short, controlled return just over the net. | Can flip the rally — you become the attacker! |
| **Flat drive counter** | Fast, flat return past the front player. | High risk/reward. If it passes the front player, rotate to attack. |
| **Cross-court lift** | When opponent smashes cross-court, lift straight. Forces them to run. | Good variation. |
| **Push to body** | Aim at the smasher's hip or chest. | Jams their next shot. |

### Key Defensive Principles

1. **Block, don't just lift.** Tight blocks at the net are the #1 way to transition from defense to attack.
2. **Stay patient.** Don't panic and try hero shots. Wait for a weak attack, then counter.
3. **Mix pace.** Drives, blocks, and lifts at different speeds make it harder for the attacker.
4. **Shift toward the smash.** Both defenders lean slightly toward the side the smash is coming from. The straight smash is fastest — cover it first.
5. **Keep low.** Bent knees, weight on the balls of your feet, racket up at waist height.

---

## 4. Targeting Weaknesses

### Opponent Analysis

- **Weaker player:** Direct more shots (especially smashes) toward the weaker defender. Force them to play under pressure.
- **Backhand side:** Most players have a weaker backhand. Target it with drives and pushes.
- **The gap between players:** Smash or push between both opponents. This creates hesitation about who should take the shot.
- **Slower player:** Use drops and net shots to force them forward, then clear behind them.
- **Player with poor footwork:** Move them corner to corner. Alternate front-back shots.

### Exploiting Formation Gaps

- If opponents stay in front-back too long → play wide shots to expose the sides.
- If opponents stay in side-by-side too long → play drops and net shots to pull one forward.
- If one opponent doesn't rotate → hit to the empty area they should be covering.

---

## 5. Tempo Control

### Playing Fast

- Use drives and flat exchanges to speed up the rally.
- Works well when you're fitter or have faster reflexes.
- Push serves, quick pushes, and rapid smash sequences.

### Playing Slow

- Use drops, net shots, and high clears to slow the rally.
- Works well against opponents who thrive on pace.
- Force them to generate their own power.

### Changing Tempo

The most effective tactic: **alternate between fast and slow.** A half-smash after three full smashes. A slow drop after a series of drives. A sudden flat push after several net rallies. Unpredictability is your greatest weapon.

---

## 6. Communication

### Before the Serve

- Hand signals for serve type and placement.
- "I'll take net" / "Watch the flick."
- Agree on general strategy ("play more on their backhand").

### During the Rally

- **"Mine!" / "Yours!"** — prevents collisions and hesitation.
- **"Cross!"** — informs partner about attack direction.
- **"Leave!"** — shuttle is going out.
- **"Up!"** — we're lifting, shift to defense.

### Between Points

- Short tactical feedback: "Their forehand defense is weak."
- Encouragement: "Great shot!" / "No worries, next point."
- **Never go silent.** Silence kills partnerships faster than bad shots.

---

## 7. Game Management

### Scoring Momentum

- When you're on a run, keep the pressure up. Don't change what's working.
- When opponent is on a run, slow down. Take your time between serves. Talk to your partner. Break their rhythm.

### Third Game (Decider)

- **Change of ends at 11 points** — adapt quickly to new conditions (light, wind, background).
- Fitness matters most in the third game. The fitter pair often wins close deciding sets.
- Keep communication positive. Morale wins third games.

### Timeouts

- 60 seconds at 11 points in every game. Use it to regroup, drink water, discuss strategy.
- 120 seconds between games. Plan adjustments.

---

## 8. Common Tactical Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Bad | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Always lifting in defense | You never get the attack back | Practice blocks and drives |
| Cross-court smashing too often | Slower, easier to defend, opens your court | Default to straight smash |
| Serving the same way every time | Opponent reads you and attacks early | Vary: short, flick, wide, T |
| Not communicating | Confusion, collisions, missed shots | Talk before, during, and after every point |
| Both players chasing the same shot | Leaves half the court empty | Clear roles: "Mine!" / "Yours!" |
| Attacking from a bad position | Off-balance smash = weak, risky | If you can't smash well, play a drop or clear |
| Ignoring the weaker opponent | Missing easy tactical opportunities | Target weaknesses deliberately |
| Standing still after hitting | Opponent plays into empty space | Move after every single shot |
