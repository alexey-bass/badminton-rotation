# Badminton Doubles — Serves

## Why the Serve Matters

The serve is the **only shot you have complete control over.** No opponent pressure, no time constraint — just you and the shuttle. Yet service errors account for a significant percentage of lost points at amateur level. In professional doubles, **30–40% of rallies end within the first 4 shots**, and it starts with the serve. A weak serve hands the initiative to your opponent. A good serve keeps you in control from the very first shot.

---

## 1. Service Rules (Quick Reference)

### Legal Serve Requirements

- The shuttle must be struck **below 1.15 meters** from the court surface (fixed height rule since 2018).
- The racket shaft must be pointing **downward** at the moment of contact — i.e., the racket head is below the hand.
- The motion must be **continuous and upward** — you cannot fake or pause mid-serve.
- Both feet must be **in contact with the floor** (no jumping) and must not be touching any court lines.
- You must strike the **cork** of the shuttle, not the feathers.
- The shuttle must fly **diagonally** into the correct service box on the opponent's side.

### Doubles Service Court

- **During the serve:** the court is **wide** (side tramlines IN) but **short** (back tramline OUT — the shuttle must land before the back doubles service line).
- **After the serve:** the full court is in play (all lines IN).
- This short-and-wide service area makes the low serve dominant in doubles.

### Even/Odd Rule

- Server's score is **even** (0, 2, 4…) → serve from the **right** service court.
- Server's score is **odd** (1, 3, 5…) → serve from the **left** service court.
- The receiver stands **diagonally opposite** the server.

## 2. The Four Serve Types

### Overview

| Serve | Trajectory | Speed | Primary Use | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Low (short)** | Low and flat, just over the net | Slow | Default serve in doubles | 80–90% |
| **Flick** | Medium height, to the back of service court | Medium | Surprise variation | 10–15% |
| **Drive** | Flat and fast, straight at opponent | Fast | Rare surprise weapon | 2–5% |
| **High** | High arc, deep to baseline | Slow | Singles only (NOT for doubles) | 0% in doubles |

## 3. Low Serve (Short Serve)

### The Default Doubles Serve

This is your bread and butter. The low serve skims just over the net and lands just past the short service line. Its purpose is to **prevent the receiver from attacking** by keeping the shuttle low.

### Trajectory

```
  Server                          Receiver
    ●                               ●
    │                               │
    │     ~~~~ just over net ~~~~   │
    │   ↗                       ↘  │
    ● ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ●
         NET                    Service
                                 line
```

The shuttle should start falling **immediately after crossing the net.** This is what makes the low serve so difficult to attack — by the time the receiver reaches it, it's already below net height.

### Technique (Backhand — Most Common)

1. **Grip:** backhand grip, slightly short on the handle for more control.
2. **Hold the shuttle:** with thumb and index finger, cork facing downward at ~45 degrees.
3. **Stance:** racket foot forward (or feet parallel), close to the service line. Weight slightly on the back foot.
4. **Backswing:** minimal — the shorter the backswing, the harder it is to read.
5. **Motion:** push the racket forward with thumb pressure and a gentle wrist flick. Shift weight from back foot to front foot.
6. **Contact point:** in front of your body, below waist height.
7. **Follow-through:** short and compact. Get ready for the next shot immediately.

### Placement Targets

```
            NET
  ┌─────────────────────────┐
  │                         │
  │   ┌─────────────────┐   │
  │   │  ①    ②    ③   │   │   ① = Wide (to tramline)
  │   │                 │   │   ② = T (center line junction)
  │   │  Service court  │   │   ③ = Wide (other side)
  │   └─────────────────┘   │
  │                         │
```

- **To the T (②):** the default target. The receiver must decide between forehand and backhand grip, causing hesitation. The angles of return are also limited.
- **Wide (① or ③):** occasional variation. Opens up your court to straight returns but forces the receiver to stretch. Use sparingly.

### Key Points

- Stand **as close to the service line as possible** — shortest shuttle travel time means less time for the receiver to react.
- The shuttle should travel in a **flat arc**, not rise and fall.
- **Consistency is everything.** 50–100 practice serves per session builds muscle memory.
- A loose, short serve (too high or too short) will be punished with a net kill.

## 4. Flick Serve

### The Mind Game Serve

The flick serve looks exactly like a low serve in setup but sends the shuttle quickly to the back of the service court, above the receiver's reach. Its purpose is **not** to win the point outright but to keep the receiver honest and prevent them from creeping forward to dominate your low serve.

### Trajectory

```
  Server                          Receiver
    ●                               ●
    │            ╱  ╲               │
    │          ╱      ╲             │
    │        ╱          ╲           │
    │     ╱ above head    ╲        │
    ● ──╱── NET ── ── ── ──╲── ── ●
                            Doubles
                            service
                            line
```

The shuttle goes **above the receiver's reach** but not as high as a singles high serve. It should arrive quickly and fall steeply near the back doubles service line.

### Technique (Backhand)

1. **Setup must be identical to the low serve.** Same stance, same grip position, same body language. This is where the deception comes from.
2. Just before contact, **push harder with the thumb** and snap the wrist to generate more power.
3. The shuttle goes higher and deeper than the low serve.
4. **Follow-through** is slightly longer than the low serve but still compact.

### Placement Targets

- **Wide (to the tramline):** forces the receiver to move further. This is generally the better option — the receiver attacks from a corner rather than the center, making defense easier.
- **Straight (down the center line):** pushes the receiver directly backward into their partner. Can cause confusion and collision.
- **To the body:** directly at the receiver's chest/shoulder. Jams their movement.

### When to Use

- After several consecutive low serves — the receiver starts creeping forward.
- When the receiver's racket position is very low and forward (anticipating a low serve).
- **Occasionally** — maybe 1 in 7–10 serves. If you flick too often, the receiver will simply stand further back and smash everything.
- Never as your default serve. The flick serve only works because the low serve exists.

### Risks

- A well-prepared receiver can **smash** a poorly executed flick.
- If the flick doesn't reach the back line, it becomes an easy attacking opportunity for the receiver.
- Overuse makes it predictable and easy to punish.

## 5. Drive Serve

### The Shock Weapon

The drive serve is a flat, fast serve hit directly at the receiver's body or backhand. It's the rarest serve in doubles but can be devastating when used at the right moment.

### Trajectory

```
  Server ─────── flat & fast ─────── Receiver
    ●  ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━  ●
              just over NET        (at body
                                    level)
```

The shuttle travels fast and flat, barely clearing the net, and arrives at the receiver before they can set up a proper return.

### Technique

1. **Setup looks like a low serve** — same stance, same preparation.
2. At the last moment, use a **stronger wrist flick and forearm rotation** to generate speed.
3. Short backswing, short follow-through — deception is key.
4. The shuttle must clear the net with minimal height.

### When to Use

- When the receiver has adapted to both your low and flick serves.
- Against **tall players** or players with **slow reactions** — a flat, fast serve at their body jams their movement.
- Against receivers who stand very close to the service line — the shuttle arrives before they can react.
- **Very rarely** — maybe 1 in 20+ serves. The element of surprise is the entire point.

### Targets

- **At the body** (hip/chest): hardest to return because the receiver can't extend their arm.
- **Backhand side:** most players' weaker side for quick reactions.
- **Wide:** risky — gives the receiver better angles if they manage to return it.

### Risks

- If the receiver is expecting it, they can drive it back or even counter-attack.
- If it hits the net, you lose the point.
- Modern service rules (1.15m height limit) make this easier than under the old rules, but it's still a high-risk serve.

---

## 6. High Serve

### NOT for Doubles

The high serve sends the shuttle on a high arc deep to the baseline. It is a **singles-only serve.** In doubles, the service court is shorter (back doubles line), and skilled receivers will smash a high serve with devastating effect. **Do not use the high serve in competitive doubles.**

The only exception: very casual recreational doubles where opponents can't smash effectively.

## 7. Partner Positioning After Each Serve Type

| Serve Type | Server After Serving | Partner Position |
|------------|---------------------|------------------|
| **Low serve** | Stay at net → front player | Mid-court, ready for third shot |
| **Flick serve (wide)** | Depending on return; may stay net or slide to one side | Cover the straight return side |
| **Flick serve (center)** | Stay roughly central | Slightly back, ready for smash return |
| **Drive serve** | Ready for fast return, may need to duck or cover | Mid-court, ready to react |

---

## 8. Hand Signals

Professional players use hand signals (behind the back, hidden from opponents) to communicate serve intentions to their partner.

### Common Signal System

- **One finger:** low serve to the T.
- **Two fingers:** low serve wide.
- **Open palm / fist:** flick serve.
- **Pointing direction:** indicates which side the flick will go.

The specific signals don't matter — what matters is that **both partners agree** on a system and use it consistently. The partner's ability to anticipate the third shot depends entirely on knowing where the serve will go.

## 9. Serve Return Positioning

### As the Receiver

- Stand close to the service line, weight forward, racket up high (above net height).
- Non-racket foot in front, racket foot behind.
- Ready to lunge forward for a low serve or jump backward for a flick.

### Priority Order for Returns

| Serve Quality | Best Return | Goal |
|---------------|------------|------|
| Loose low serve (above net) | **Net kill** | Win the point immediately |
| Good low serve (at net height) | **Push** to mid-court or **tight net shot** | Force a weak third shot |
| Very good low serve (below net) | **Lift** (high and deep) | Last resort — survive and defend |
| Flick serve (reached early) | **Attacking drop or smash** | Take the initiative |
| Flick serve (reached late) | **Clear** (high and deep) | Reset and defend |
| Drive serve | **Block or counter-drive** | Neutralize and compete for attack |

### Golden Rule for Serve Returns

**Do not lift unless you have no other option.** Every lift hands the attack to the serving side. Push, drive, net — anything that keeps the shuttle going down or flat is better than a lift.

## 10. Common Serving Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Bad | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Serve too high | Receiver kills it at the net | Practice lower trajectory, barely over the net |
| Serve too short | Doesn't reach the service line = fault | Aim for the line, not in front of it |
| Standing too far from service line | Gives receiver more time to react | Stand as close to the line as possible |
| Same serve every time | Receiver reads you and attacks | Vary: T, wide, flick, occasionally drive |
| Flicking too often | Receiver stands back and smashes | Flick max 10–15% of the time |
| No hand signals | Partner doesn't know where to position | Agree on a signal system and use it |
| Rushing the serve | Poor quality, errors | Take your time — it's the one shot with no pressure |
| Tensing up | Loss of control and feel | Relaxed grip, smooth motion |
