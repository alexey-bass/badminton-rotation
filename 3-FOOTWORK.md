# Badminton Doubles — Footwork

## Why Footwork Matters

Power, technique, and tactics are useless if you can't reach the shuttle in time. Good footwork gives you: **time** (arrive earlier, make better decisions), **balance** (stable body = better shots), **efficiency** (save energy over long matches), and **reach** (cover more court with fewer steps). In doubles, you cover less court than singles, but the shuttle arrives **much faster**, so explosive short-range movement is critical.

---

## 1. Base Position

### What Is It?

The base position is your "home" — the spot you return to after every shot. It's the optimal position to cover the area you're responsible for.

### Doubles Base Positions

Unlike singles (where the base is center court), doubles base positions change depending on your formation:

| Formation | Your Role | Base Position |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Attack (front-back) | Front player | Just behind service line, shifted toward the attack side |
| Attack (front-back) | Rear player | Center of rear court, slightly behind mid-court |
| Defense (side-by-side) | Either player | Center of your half, ~1 step behind mid-court |
| Serving | Server | Close to service line |
| Serving | Partner | Mid-court, ~2 racket lengths behind server |

### The Rule

After every shot, **return to your base position immediately.** Do not admire your shot. Do not stand where you hit. Move back to base.

## 2. Ready Position (Stance)

The ready position is your body posture at the base position, prepared to move in any direction.

### Attacking Ready Position

- Feet shoulder-width apart or slightly wider.
- **Racket leg behind**, non-racket leg forward.
- Weight slightly forward, on the balls of the feet.
- Racket up — head at or above net height (front player) or shoulder height (rear player).
- Knees slightly bent.
- **Purpose:** you're anticipating a defensive return and preparing to attack.

### Defensive Ready Position

- Feet wider than shoulder-width, roughly parallel.
- Weight low — deep knee bend.
- Racket at **waist height** or slightly higher.
- **Loose grip** — allows fast wrist reactions to fast smashes.
- Non-racket arm up for balance (don't let it hang dead).
- **Purpose:** you're anticipating a smash and need maximum reaction time.

## 3. The Split Step

The most important single movement in badminton. A small downward "hop" or "bounce" timed to your opponent's contact with the shuttle.

### How to Do It

1. As your opponent is about to hit the shuttle, perform a small hop/bounce.
2. Land with both feet roughly shoulder-width apart, knees bent.
3. The landing loads your legs like a spring — you can explode in any direction.
4. Push off with the foot **opposite** to your intended direction.

### Timing

- The split step happens **just before** or **at the moment** your opponent hits the shuttle.
- Too early = you land and lose the spring before you know where to go.
- Too late = you're mid-hop when you should already be moving.

### Where to Use It

- **Every time** your opponent is about to hit the shuttle.
- Before returning serve.
- At your base position between shots.
- Even at the net as the front player — a small micro-split keeps you reactive.

### Common Mistake

Skipping the split step. Many players just stand flat-footed and then react. The split step gives you a measurable speed advantage in the first 1-2 steps.

## 4. Movement Types

### Lunge

The most common movement in doubles. Used for short distances, especially at the front court.

**Technique:**
- Push off with the rear foot.
- Step forward with the racket leg (the leg on the same side as your racket hand).
- Bend the front knee deeply (knee should not extend past the toes).
- Back leg stays extended for balance and push-back.
- **Recovery:** push back off the front foot to return to base.

**Used for:** net shots, net kills, front court intercepts, short pushes.

### Chasse Step (Shuffle Step)

A sideways sliding movement where your feet don't cross. Efficient for lateral movement.

**Technique:**
1. Push off with the foot farthest from your destination.
2. Slide the leading foot toward the target.
3. Bring the trailing foot toward the leading foot (but don't cross).
4. Repeat if needed.

**Used for:** defensive lateral movement, adjusting position at the net, mid-court coverage.

### Running Steps

Used for covering longer distances quickly.

**Technique:**
- Standard running motion but ending with the racket foot forward.
- Used when you're caught far from the shuttle.
- Less controlled than chasse steps but faster for long distances.

**Used for:** chasing clears to the back corner, recovering from an extreme position.

### Cross Step

One foot crosses in front of or behind the other. Used for reaching wider or covering more ground quickly.

**Technique:**
- The non-racket foot crosses in front of the racket foot.
- Allows larger strides than chasse steps.

**Used for:** moving to the rear court, wide defensive reaches.

### Scissor Kick (Jump)

A rotational jump used in the rear court for overhead shots (smash, drop, clear).

**Technique:**
1. From a sideways stance, load weight on the racket leg.
2. Push off and jump, rotating your body in the air.
3. Land with the **racket leg in front**, non-racket leg behind.
4. The rotation transfers your weight forward, helping recovery.

**Used for:** smashes, clears, drops from the rear court. Without the scissor kick, you tend to fall backward after overhead shots, slowing recovery.

### China Jump (Tap Jump)

A quick jump used at the net for intercepting high shuttles.

**Technique:**
- A short, explosive upward jump from both feet (or mainly the non-racket foot).
- Arms and racket extend upward.
- Land and recover quickly.

**Used for:** net kills, high intercepts at the front court.

## 5. Court Zones & Movement Patterns

### The Six Corners

The court has six key target areas you must be able to reach:

```
            NET
  ┌─────────────────────────┐
  │  1. Front    2. Front   │
  │     Left        Right   │
  │                         │
  │  3. Mid      4. Mid     │
  │     Left        Right   │
  │                         │
  │  5. Rear     6. Rear    │
  │     Left        Right   │
  └─────────────────────────┘
```

### Doubles-Specific Patterns

In doubles, your movement patterns are shorter but need to be faster:

**Front player patterns:**
- Base → front left → base → front right (lateral net coverage).
- Base → mid-court intercept → base.
- Base → lunge for net kill → recovery.

**Rear player patterns:**
- Base → rear right smash → base.
- Base → rear left overhead → base.
- Base → follow drop shot forward (rotate to front).

**Defensive patterns:**
- Base → wide right defense → base → wide left defense.
- Low lateral shuffles covering your half of the court.

## 6. Doubles-Specific Footwork

### Front Court Footwork

- Movements are short — usually 1-2 steps maximum.
- **Lunge is king.** Split step → chasse → lunge covers most front court situations.
- Racket foot leads when moving forward.
- Stay light on your feet — small bounces keep you reactive.
- After playing a net shot, **don't retreat**. Hold your position or move slightly forward to threaten the net kill.

### Rear Court Footwork

- Getting behind the shuttle is critical for powerful overhead shots.
- **Scissor kick** for forehand overhead shots — provides power and forward recovery.
- For backhand rear court (the hardest area): turn body, use cross step, and play the shot. Recovery is slower — your partner must be ready to cover.
- After smashing: recover to base, don't fall backward.

### Defensive Footwork

- Low, wide stance — feet wider than shoulder-width.
- Chasse steps (shuffles) for lateral movement.
- **React to the sound** of the smash, not just the visual — it's faster.
- No big steps — short, quick adjustments.
- Split step before each smash arrives.

### Rotation Footwork

- When rotating from side-by-side to front-back: the player moving forward uses **1-2 quick steps** toward the net. The player moving back uses **cross steps** or **chasse steps** backward.
- When rotating from front-back to side-by-side: the front player uses **backward chasse steps** to their side. The rear player slides to the opposite side.
- **Don't turn your back to the net** during rotation — always face forward or sideways.

---

## 7. Recovery

Recovery is the movement **back to your base position** after hitting a shot. Good recovery is what allows you to be ready for the next shot.

### Recovery Principles

1. **Push off the front foot** after a lunge to return quickly.
2. **Use the scissor kick** in the rear court to keep forward momentum after overhead shots.
3. **Don't take extra steps** — find the most efficient path back to base.
4. **Start recovering immediately** after hitting — don't watch where your shot goes.
5. **Small adjustment steps** as you return — keep your feet active, don't coast.

## 8. Footwork Drills for Doubles

### Shadow Footwork (No Shuttle)

Move to each of the 6 corners from your base position and back. Focus on correct technique, not speed. 30 seconds on, 30 seconds rest × 4 rounds.

### Reaction Footwork

Partner or coach calls out positions ("front left!", "rear right!"). React and move with correct footwork, return to base each time. 30 seconds on, 30 seconds rest × 5 rounds.

### Defensive Lateral Drill

Stand in defensive position. Partner feeds smashes or drives alternating left and right. Focus on low stance, chasse steps, and quick racket work. 2 minutes per set.

### Multi-Shuttle Feeding

Coach feeds shuttles rapidly to different areas. Player must use correct footwork to reach each one. Builds stamina and automatic movement patterns.

### Net T's (Front Court Speed)

Both players at opposite sides of the net, T of the service line. Fast, controlled exchanges. Focus on quick, light feet and rapid grip changes.

## 9. Common Footwork Mistakes

| Mistake | Effect | Fix |
|---------|--------|-----|
| No split step | Slow first movement, always late | Split step before every opponent's shot |
| Flat-footed stance | Can't react quickly | Stay on the balls of your feet |
| Standing up straight | Slow reactions, poor balance | Bend your knees, stay low |
| Wrong foot leading the lunge | Weak position, poor recovery | Racket foot always leads forward |
| Not recovering to base | Out of position for next shot | Move back immediately after hitting |
| Turning back to the net | Can't see opponent's shot | Stay facing forward or sideways |
| Taking too many steps | Slow, wastes energy | Minimize steps: split → chasse → lunge |
| Falling backward after overhead shots | Slow recovery, vulnerable | Use scissor kick to maintain forward momentum |
